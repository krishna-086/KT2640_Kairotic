The Core Problem

With misinformation creeping in like creatures from the Upside Down, it becomes harder for citizens to tell what's real and what's just another distorted tale. Fake reports about supernatural events, missing people, or secret experiments can cause panic and confusion across the community.

Why Existing Solutions Fail

  • ‚úï
    Black-box classifiers provide binary labels with no explanation, making it impossible to understand or verify their decisions.
  • ‚úï
    Human fact-checkers cannot scale to the volume of content being produced every second.
  • ‚úï
    Keyword blocklists are easily circumvented by slightly modifying language patterns.
  • ‚úï
    Single-signal systems miss the nuance required for accurate credibility assessment.
Our Mission: Design an intelligent "Hawkins Truth Engine" that analyzes news articles, social media posts, and URLs to determine whether a story belongs to the Real World or the Upside Down. The system must not only classify, but explain why a story appears suspicious using linguistic, statistical, and source-based evidence.

Document Ingestion & Preprocessing

Raw input (text, URL, or social post) is transformed into a structured Document. For URLs, we fetch the page content with size limits and timeout protection, then extract clean text using trafilatura with BeautifulSoup fallback.

The text is broken into sentences and tokens with precise character positions, enabling exact highlighting in the UI. We detect named entities (people, organizations) using capitalization patterns and find quote attributions using regex.

httpx trafilatura BeautifulSoup langdetect
2

Linguistic Pattern Analysis

Scans for writing patterns commonly found in misinformation: clickbait punctuation (excessive ! and ?), ALL-CAPS shouting, clickbait phrases ("you won't believe"), conspiracy language ("they don't want you to know"), urgency words, and anonymous authority citations ("experts say" without naming them).

Each pattern has a severity level and contributes to the linguistic risk score. The exact phrases detected are recorded with their positions for UI highlighting.

Statistical Pattern Analysis

Examines mathematical properties of text structure: lexical diversity (vocabulary richness), repetition ratio (keyword stuffing), sentence length uniformity (artificial generation indicator), and token entropy (word distribution irregularities).

Natural human writing has predictable statistical patterns. Deviations suggest templated content, bot generation, or SEO manipulation.

numpy scikit-learn Entropy Calculation
4

Source Intelligence Gathering

For URL inputs, queries RDAP (Registration Data Access Protocol) to get domain registration information. Young domains (<90 days) are penalized; established domains (>1 year) get a trust bonus. Domains on "hold" status are flagged as suspicious.

Also checks document metadata for missing author and publication date, both of which are credibility indicators.

Claim Extraction & Verification

Identifies factual claims in the text (up to 12) and classifies them as factual, speculative, predictive, or opinion-presented-as-fact. For medical topics (detected via 28+ trigger terms), searches PubMed for scientific literature. For general claims, searches GDELT news archive.

Each claim is labeled: supported (‚â•2 citations), unsupported (strong claim, no backing), unverifiable (no relevant results), or contested (conflicting evidence).

PubMed E-utilities GDELT DOC API rapidfuzz
6

Deterministic Reasoning & Verdict

Combines all signals using explicit rules: base risk = 55% linguistic + 45% statistical. Source trust gates the risk (amplified for untrusted, dampened for trusted). Claim support adjusts the score. Three explicit rules handle edge cases.

Final credibility score (0-100) maps to verdict: Likely Real (‚â•70), Suspicious (40-69), Likely Fake (<40). World label is binary: "Real World" for Likely Real, "Upside Down" otherwise.

Frequently Asked Questions

Detailed answers to common questions

Does this system guarantee truth or prove something is fake? +
No, and this is by design. The Hawkins Truth Engine is explicitly NOT a truth oracle. Here's why:
  • Truth is complex: Determining absolute truth often requires expert knowledge, context, and time that automated systems cannot replicate.
  • Evidence vs. verdict: We provide evidence-backed signals and an interpretable assessment. The final judgment remains with human reviewers.
  • Conservative labeling: "Unsupported" means we couldn't find backing evidence ‚Äî it does NOT mean the claim is false. The claim might be too new, too niche, or simply not in our databases.
  • Uncertainty acknowledgment: When external services fail or evidence is ambiguous, we explicitly flag this uncertainty rather than hiding it.
The system is designed as a triage tool to help human reviewers prioritize what to investigate, not to replace human judgment.
What does "unsupported" mean for a claim? +
"Unsupported" is a conservative internal label with a specific meaning:
  • Strong framing detected: The claim uses certainty language (e.g., "100% guaranteed", "cures all", "proven fact").
  • No attribution found: The claim doesn't cite a named source, expert, or study.
  • No external backing: Our searches of PubMed (for medical claims) and GDELT (for news claims) returned no supporting evidence.
Important: "Unsupported" does NOT mean "false." The claim might be:
  • Too recent to appear in databases
  • From a niche domain not covered by our sources
  • True but poorly attributed in the source text
We never treat absence of evidence as evidence of falsity.
Why "Real World" vs "Upside Down" labels? +
These thematic labels are inspired by the problem prompt (the town of Hawkins, Stranger Things references) and serve two purposes:
  • Engaging presentation: Technical verdicts can feel cold and confusing. The thematic labels make the output more memorable and intuitive.
  • Quick triage: Binary classification is useful for rapid sorting. "Upside Down" = investigate further; "Real World" = likely credible.
The mapping is simple:
  • Real World = "Likely Real" verdict (credibility score ‚â•70)
  • Upside Down = "Suspicious" or "Likely Fake" verdict (score <70)
The underlying tri-verdict system (Likely Real / Suspicious / Likely Fake) provides more nuance for detailed review, while the binary world label enables quick assessment.
What happens when external services fail? +
The system is designed for graceful degradation:
  • Processing continues: If RDAP, PubMed, or GDELT is unavailable, the system continues with evidence from other analyzers.
  • Explicit uncertainty flags: The failure is recorded as an uncertainty flag (e.g., "ncbi_unavailable", "rdap_unavailable") visible in the output.
  • Confidence dampening: When uncertainty flags are present, the confidence score is automatically capped at 75% to reflect reduced certainty.
  • Reasoning path visibility: The reasoning_path in the output shows exactly which evidence sources were and weren't available.
This approach ensures the system remains useful even when external dependencies are temporarily unavailable, while being transparent about reduced confidence.
Why not use a machine learning classifier? +
This was a deliberate design choice prioritizing explainability:
  • Black box problem: ML classifiers can achieve high accuracy but users cannot understand why content was flagged. This erodes trust.
  • Auditability: Our deterministic rules can be examined, challenged, and modified. Every decision is traceable through explicit rules in the code.
  • No training data bias: ML models learn biases from their training data. Our rules are explicit and can be audited for bias.
  • No distribution shift: ML models degrade when content patterns change. Rule-based systems adapt through rule updates.
  • Provenance tracking: Every evidence item has a unique ID, source module, and pointers back to the original text. ML embeddings don't provide this traceability.
Trade-off acknowledged: This approach may miss some subtle patterns that ML would catch. We accept this trade-off because explainability is more important than marginal accuracy gains for credibility assessment.
How accurate is the confidence score? +
The confidence score is explicitly marked as uncalibrated. Here's what that means:
  • Heuristic calculation: Confidence is computed from agreement between analyzers (35%), evidence coverage (30%), and a base value (35%).
  • Not a probability: A 70% confidence does NOT mean "70% chance of being correct." It's a relative indicator of certainty.
  • No ground truth calibration: Proper calibration requires labeled datasets to verify that X% confidence corresponds to X% accuracy. This POC hasn't been calibrated against such data.
How to interpret it:
  • High confidence + multiple evidence sources = stronger assessment
  • Low confidence or uncertainty flags = human review especially important
  • Don't treat it as a probability; treat it as "how much evidence supports this verdict"
What are the main limitations of this system? +
We are transparent about limitations:
  • English-first: Claim extraction and linguistic patterns are optimized for English. Other languages may have reduced accuracy.
  • No image/video analysis: The system only analyzes text. Manipulated images or deepfakes are not detected.
  • External service dependencies: GDELT, PubMed, and RDAP may be unavailable, rate-limited, or incomplete.
  • Heuristic NER: Named entity recognition uses simple patterns, not a trained NER model. May miss or misclassify entities.
  • No real-time fact database: Breaking news may not yet appear in our evidence sources.
  • Confidence not calibrated: The confidence score is a heuristic, not a calibrated probability.
Future extensions could address these: multi-language support, image analysis, confidence calibration, and additional evidence sources.
